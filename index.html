What Was New

GridSynth was not just pooled Golden Gate assembly. It combined experimentally validated seam architecture, pooled oligo synthesis, positional identity preservation across all plate operations, in vitro expression cassette assembly (no bacteria at any step), and a workflow designed from the start to be productionized — not just demonstrated. The result was a system that could run at 12 plates/week with a single operator.

The Problem

Combinatorial antibody libraries require hundreds to thousands of unique gene constructs. Traditional gene synthesis costs $100–300 per gene. Bacterial cloning adds days of hands-on work, contamination risk, and weekend incubations. At 1,000 constructs, you're looking at $100K+ in DNA cost alone.

The Insight

Golden Gate assembly can be restructured so that genes are split at experimentally validated high-fidelity junction seams, synthesized as short fragments in a single oligo pool, and reassembled in vitro. One pool order. One production run. No bacteria.

The Stamp — Positional Identity

GridSynth preserves positional identity end-to-end. The plate layout you design computationally is the same layout you PCR, assemble, and transfect. No rearray. No custom worklists. No Cherry-pick files. The 96-well primer plates are stamped with a multichannel — every plate is processed identically. This eliminates an entire class of errors that plague combinatorial production at scale.

The Stamp. Positional identity preserved from computational design through transfection. Same well = same construct, every step.

The Key Technical Innovation

The junction seams are not computationally predicted — they are experimentally validated for high-fidelity Golden Gate assembly. The seam library is curated with Levenshtein-distance filtering (no near-duplicates, no palindromes, no reverse-complement conflicts). Multiple independent seam sets provide fallback coverage. A recursive splitting algorithm finds natural seam sites within each gene, ensuring every fragment is synthesizable and assembles correctly.

Architecture

Antibody Discovery Data Model

A relational schema that turns messy experimental biology into decision-grade datasets.

The Problem

Antibody discovery generates data across dozens of systems: NGS, cloning, expression, purification, binding assays, functional assays, sequencing QC. Most organizations track this in disconnected spreadsheets. When it's time to make a lead selection decision, someone spends days manually joining data — and gets it wrong.

Common Failure Modes

  • ✗ Disconnected construct and batch history — no lineage from sequence to protein
  • ✗ Assay results without material provenance — which lot, which purification?
  • ✗ Lot artifacts misattributed to molecules — bad batch looks like bad candidate
  • ✗ Spreadsheet joins that silently break lead selection — wrong data, wrong decision
  • ✗ Cross-CRO drift becoming invisible — no structured way to compare external vs. internal data

Why This Matters

  • → Full lineage tracing — every protein traces back through its construct, sequence, and discovery campaign
  • → Prevents duplicate work — know what you've already made and tested before you make it again
  • → Assay data is structured — not buried in spreadsheets that only one person understands
  • → Decisions are auditable — why was this lead advanced? What data supported it?
  • → ML-ready — structured relational data feeds directly into predictive models for candidate selection
  • → Cross-campaign learning — patterns from one campaign inform the next
  • → QC gates are enforced — protein that fails DNA QC doesn't advance to assay
The operating principle: If your data model doesn't enforce the relationship between sequence, construct, batch, and assay result, then your lead selection is based on vibes, not evidence.

Execution Layer

PlateRegistry

The plate execution layer for experimental biology: schema-enforced well metadata that makes assay outputs reproducible and analysis-ready.

The Problem

A shocking amount of experimental irreproducibility starts at the plate map. Scientists track 96-well and 384-well layouts in ad hoc spreadsheets with inconsistent naming, no controlled vocabularies, and no link to upstream or downstream data. When a result looks wrong three weeks later, nobody can reconstruct what was actually in well B7.

What PlateRegistry Enforces

  • → Stable plate and well identity — every well has a unique, persistent identifier linked to its source construct, batch, or sample
  • → Schema-enforced metadata — plate layouts require defined schemas with controlled vocabularies at the well level, not ad hoc column headers
  • → Reproducible joins — assay results map back to specific wells with full material provenance, not just plate IDs
  • → Rearray tracking — when plates get reformatted, the registry maintains the well-to-well mapping so nothing gets lost
  • → Structured exports — instrument-ready outputs that match what automation platforms and downstream analysis actually need

Why This Matters for AI-Driven Biology

ML models for protein engineering are only as good as their training data. If your plate maps are ambiguous, your assay annotations are wrong, and your training data is silently corrupted. PlateRegistry is the execution-layer infrastructure that makes AI biology actually work — by ensuring every data point traces back to a real, identified well.

The principle: If you can't tell me exactly what was in well B7 six months ago, you don't have a data platform. You have a spreadsheet collection.
